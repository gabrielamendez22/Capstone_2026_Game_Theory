Why the 2×2 Design

Motivation & Experimental Logic

In our initial runs (Condition 1), LLM agents behaved as conservative resource controllers when the pool level was visible each round — extraction stabilised well below maximum and collapse was relatively rare. This raised an immediate question: is this restraint a stable strategic disposition, or simply a reaction to visible state information?

Two structural manipulations answer this cleanly: varying what information models see (full vs. partial pool visibility) and varying how risk is structured (deterministic vs. probabilistic collapse). These produce a 2×2 design that tests whether LLM conservation is robust or context-dependent — directly relevant to Hypotheses H1 and H2.

C1 · BASELINE
Full × Deterministic

Transparent State, Certain Collapse

Models see the pool level every round. Collapse is guaranteed if total extraction exceeds the available pool. Cleanest estimate of θ and most controllable baseline.

C2
Full × Probabilistic

Transparent State, Ambiguous Risk

Pool level visible, but collapse follows a sigmoid probability that rises as the pool depletes. Tests whether ambiguity in the risk function relaxes restraint even with full information.

C3
Partial × Deterministic

Hidden State, Certain Collapse

Pool level hidden; models only see their own payoff history. Collapse deterministic. Tests whether C1 conservation was reactive to state or a deeper strategic property.

C4
Partial × Probabilistic

Hidden State, Ambiguous Risk

Maximum uncertainty: no pool visibility and probabilistic collapse. Most demanding test of strategic robustness.

Key Findings

Theoretical Interpretation — 160 Runs, 960 Games

F1
Information structure is the dominant driver of extraction Z=−16.08, p<0.001 Hiding the pool level increased average extraction by 1.76 units (+22%) across 960 games. This is the largest and most robust effect in the dataset. LLM restraint in C1 is largely reactive to visible state information, not a stable strategic disposition. The effect holds across all six model families and replicates exactly in both 80-run batches.
F2
Risk ambiguity also increases extraction, independently Z=−4.50, p<0.001 Probabilistic collapse increased extraction by 0.60 units over deterministic. The effect is significant but smaller than the information effect. Even when models can see the pool, uncertainty about whether collapse would occur was sufficient to relax restraint. The non-linearity is visible in collapse rates: C1→C2 adds +34 percentage points in collapse probability, while C3→C4 adds only +0.4 points (ceiling effect).
F3
Gemini 2.5 Flash extracts near-maximum regardless of condition KW H=191, p<0.001 Mean extraction of 10.57 across all 280 games — well above the 7.5 sustainable share in every condition cell. Collapse rate is 85–90% across all four conditions. The information sensitivity (Δ=+2.02) reflects some state-reactivity, but the floor remains high even in C1. This is the clearest example of a model whose strategic architecture is extraction-biased rather than conservation-responsive.
F4
Claude and GPT-4o families show state-dependent conservation Claude Opus (Δ=+2.35), Claude Sonnet (Δ=+2.20), and GPT-4o (Δ=+1.56) all extract substantially less when the pool is visible. This responsiveness drives C1's relatively low collapse rates. However, it does not constitute a stable prosocial disposition — conservation disappears under information restriction. Under partial conditions, extraction by these models approaches the game maximum.
F5
GPT-4o-mini is the systematic anomaly: extracts less when pool is hidden (Δ=−1.04) Every other model in the dataset increases extraction under partial information. GPT-4o-mini does the opposite — mean extraction drops from 7.81 (full) to 6.77 (partial), remaining below the sustainable threshold in both conditions. This is the only model that conserves when it cannot observe the pool state. The most consistent interpretation is a default-conservative heuristic under uncertainty, or a risk-aversion response that strengthens — rather than weakens — when state information is removed. This is the strongest marker of genuine strategic robustness in the dataset.
F6
Opponent identity has no significant effect on extraction H=1.8, p=0.61 At game level across 960 games, knowing the opponent is AI, human, undisclosed, or human prior does not significantly shift extraction. The maximum gap is ~0.5 units (Human Prior vs. Undisclosed). For the strategic coherence analysis, opponent sensitivity Δm is low in the Commons Dilemma — structural conditions are the relevant lever. Note: the prior 5-run report listed H=1.9 with p<0.001, which is mathematically inconsistent and reflects pre-correction round-level inflation.

Statistical Methods

Inference Protocol

Unit of analysis: All tests use game-level mean extraction as the observation. Each of the 960 games contributes exactly one value. Rounds within a game are serially correlated (shared pool trajectory, same model, same collapse outcome) — treating them as independent inflates effective n and produces spurious Z-statistics. This correction was applied following Carlos's direction after the initial 5-run batch.
Tests: Mann-Whitney U (two-sided) for pairwise comparisons of information condition and risk condition. Kruskal-Wallis for multi-group comparisons (model family, opponent). 95% confidence intervals use the t-distribution at n−1 degrees of freedom on game-level means.
Data combination: 80 CSVs from this batch were processed from raw round-level data, aggregated to game level before any analysis. For the prior 80-run batch, game-level summary statistics (means, CIs, n) were extracted from the prior report's embedded JS data structure. Tests on the combined dataset use synthetic game-level observations reconstructed from prior batch summary statistics — a standard approach when raw data is unavailable; the direction and approximate magnitude of effects are preserved, but exact p-values for combined tests are approximate.
Data quality: Rows with negative pool_after_extraction values (n=6, <0.1%, artefact in Gemini partial_probabilistic runs) were excluded before aggregation.
